# Composition

Composition defines how Lumina visuals are structured.

A Lumina visual should feel calm, intentional and easy to understand.

The viewer should immediately recognise the main idea, then discover supporting details.

Good composition is essential because Lumina often explains complex systems such as semantic search, metadata enrichment, embeddings, AI agents, APIs and library workflows.

The composition should reduce complexity, not amplify it.

---

## Core Principle

Every Lumina visual should have one clear focal point.

The focal point may be:

- a person working with an intelligent system
- a central knowledge graph
- a search interface
- a workflow or pipeline
- a dashboard
- a set of connected document cards
- a Lumina component such as Core, Engine, Studio or Experience

Supporting elements should reinforce the focal point.

They should not compete with it.

If everything is important, nothing is important.

---

## Whitespace

Whitespace is a defining element of the Lumina visual identity.

Use whitespace to create:

- clarity
- calmness
- visual hierarchy
- room for titles
- room for explanation
- a sense of academic quality

Do not fill every available area.

Empty space helps the viewer understand the structure of the image.

A Lumina visual should feel spacious before it feels rich.

---

## Visual Hierarchy

Use clear hierarchy to guide the viewer.

A good Lumina visual has:

1. one primary element
2. two or three supporting elements
3. subtle background or context elements

The viewer should be able to understand the visual in layers:

- first: the main idea
- second: the relationships
- third: the supporting details

Avoid compositions where many small elements compete for attention.

---

## Balance

Lumina compositions should feel balanced but not rigid.

Asymmetrical layouts are often preferred because they feel modern and dynamic.

Use balance between:

- people and interfaces
- documents and data structures
- abstract systems and human context
- white space and visual density
- technical elements and library elements

Avoid layouts that feel either too empty or too crowded.

---

## Preferred Composition Patterns

### Presentation Hero

Use for opening slides, section dividers and high-level concept introductions.

Preferred structure:

- one strong visual scene
- generous whitespace for title text
- calm background
- simplified interface or knowledge structure
- optional person or small group of people
- minimal supporting elements

Typical layout:

- visual focus on the right, text space on the left
- or visual focus in the centre with large whitespace around it

Avoid dense diagrams in hero visuals.

A hero visual should communicate mood and direction, not technical detail.

---

### Web Hero

Use for landing pages, product pages or service descriptions.

Preferred structure:

- wide horizontal composition
- calm atmospheric background
- clear central visual idea
- abstract UI elements
- enough empty space for headings and calls to action
- strong but restrained ETH Blue presence

Web hero visuals may be slightly more atmospheric than presentation visuals.

They should still remain precise and calm.

Avoid making web visuals look like generic SaaS marketing.

---

### Concept Explainer

Use for explaining one idea such as semantic search, metadata enrichment or embedding-based matching.

Preferred structure:

- one central concept
- a small number of labelled or symbolic elements
- clear relationships
- simplified process or before-after structure
- visual grouping

Concept explainers should be understandable without extensive explanation.

Avoid decorative illustrations that do not explain the concept.

---

### Workflow or Pipeline

Use for processes such as metadata enrichment, subject indexing, data processing or AI-assisted review.

Preferred structure:

- left-to-right or top-to-bottom flow
- clear process steps
- document cards or data objects entering the flow
- transformation or enrichment stages
- human review checkpoint where appropriate
- final structured output

Use arrows or connection lines sparingly.

Avoid overly mechanical metaphors such as gears, machines or factory imagery.

A Lumina pipeline should feel like structured knowledge work, not industrial automation.

---

### Architecture Diagram

Use for Lumina Core, Engine, Studio, Experience and system-level explanations.

Preferred structure:

- layered system view
- clear separation between components
- simplified service blocks
- reusable modules
- clean connections
- limited number of labels
- strong visual grouping

Architecture diagrams should be abstract and readable.

They do not need to show every technical detail.

Prefer explaining relationships over showing infrastructure complexity.

Avoid server-rack imagery, cloud clutter and overly technical network diagrams.

---

### Dashboard Illustration

Use for Lumina Studio, monitoring, quality review, orchestration and operational views.

Preferred structure:

- large main dashboard panel
- smaller supporting cards
- status indicators
- charts or progress elements
- workflow overview
- human operator or expert when useful

Dashboards should look plausible but not realistic.

Avoid dense tables, tiny labels and real product screenshots.

The goal is to communicate control, overview and trust.

---

### Knowledge Network

Use for semantic relationships, knowledge graphs, collections, subjects and connected resources.

Preferred structure:

- limited number of nodes
- readable clusters
- clear central or thematic grouping
- connection lines with purpose
- document or concept cards connected to the network
- enough whitespace between clusters

Avoid chaotic network spaghetti.

A knowledge network should communicate understanding, not complexity.

---

## Placement of People

People should be placed where they clarify the human role in the system.

Useful placements include:

- next to an interface
- reviewing metadata
- exploring search results
- guiding a workflow
- collaborating around a knowledge structure
- monitoring a dashboard

People should not be used as decoration only.

A person in a Lumina visual should represent expertise, responsibility or interpretation.

Avoid placing people in generic corporate poses unrelated to the concept.

---

## Placement of Interfaces

Interfaces should usually be secondary to the concept they explain.

They may appear as:

- floating cards
- dashboard panels
- search fields
- result clusters
- metadata forms
- workflow boards
- API cards
- graph panels

Interfaces should be simplified and abstract.

Do not rely on generated text inside interfaces.

Use interface shapes to suggest function, not to reproduce a real screen.

---

## Relationship Lines

Relationship lines are important in Lumina visuals.

Use them to show:

- semantic connections
- data flows
- document relationships
- workflow progression
- API connections
- human-system interaction

Lines should be:

- clean
- thin
- purposeful
- easy to follow
- visually calm

Avoid excessive lines.

Connections should create structure, not visual noise.

---

## Backgrounds

Backgrounds should be quiet.

Preferred backgrounds:

- white
- very light ETH Blue shades
- very light ETH Petrol shades
- light grey
- subtle abstract shapes
- soft gradients

Backgrounds may contain faint structures such as:

- grid hints
- large soft circles
- abstract panels
- subtle document silhouettes
- low-contrast network fragments

Avoid busy backgrounds.

The background should support the visual, not become the visual.

---

## Aspect Ratios

Common aspect ratios:

### 16:9

Best for presentation slides.

Use generous whitespace and a clear focal point.

### Wide hero

Best for web pages.

Use horizontal flow and leave space for text overlays.

### Square

Useful for social media, thumbnails or compact concept visuals.

Keep the composition simple.

### Portrait

Useful for posters or vertical web sections.

Use stacked hierarchy and avoid overloading the vertical space.

---

## Text Space

Many Lumina visuals will be used in slides or web layouts with separate text.

Therefore, compositions should often leave room for:

- slide titles
- section headings
- captions
- short explanations
- callouts

Do not place essential visual elements too close to the edges.

Avoid filling the entire canvas with illustration.

Leave safe areas for layout adaptation.

---

## Cropping and Reuse

Lumina visuals should be reusable across different formats when possible.

Design compositions so that they can be:

- placed on a slide
- cropped for a web banner
- reused in documentation
- adapted into an infographic
- combined with other Lumina visuals

Avoid compositions that only work in one very specific layout.

---

## Density

Use different levels of density depending on the medium.

### Low density

Use for:

- presentation heroes
- web heroes
- chapter dividers
- high-level communication

### Medium density

Use for:

- concept explainers
- product visuals
- service descriptions
- workflow illustrations

### High density

Use only for:

- architecture diagrams
- technical documentation
- detailed process explanations

Even high-density visuals should remain structured and readable.

---

## Composition Checklist

A Lumina composition is successful when:

- there is one clear focal point
- the visual feels calm and spacious
- supporting elements reinforce the main idea
- relationships are readable
- the hierarchy is clear
- the image leaves room for text if needed
- people, interfaces and knowledge objects are balanced
- the result works for the intended medium
- the composition feels like part of the Lumina ecosystem

If a composition feels crowded, reduce the number of elements.

If it feels generic, strengthen the relationship between people, knowledge and intelligent library services.
